Who Is at Risk?

The leak affects virtually every major online service. If you use the internet, you are potentially at risk. The primary danger comes from password reuse across different sites.

A Blueprint for Mass Exploitation

This leak is not just a list of passwords; it's a tool for cybercriminals. With this data, they can perform several malicious activities:

  • Account Takeovers (ATO): Directly logging into your accounts to steal information or money.
  • Identity Theft: Using your personal data from multiple services to impersonate you.
  • Targeted Phishing Scams: Crafting highly convincing fake emails or texts using your real information to trick you.
  • Credential Stuffing: The most common attack, where bots automatically try your leaked password on hundreds of other websites.

Your Personal Action Plan

Follow these steps to significantly improve your digital security. Completing this checklist is the single best thing you can do right now.

0 of 6 tasks completed

1. Change Critical Passwords+

Start with your most sensitive accounts: primary email, banking, and financial services. Create a new, unique, and strong password for each. Do not reuse them!

2. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)+

MFA is your best defense. Even if criminals have your password, they can't log in without the second factor (like a code from your phone). Enable it on every service that offers it.

3. Use a Password Manager+

It's impossible to remember unique passwords for every site. A password manager generates and stores them for you securely. This is a game-changer for online safety.

4. Check If You've Been Pwned+

Use a trusted service like "Have I Been Pwned" or Google's Dark Web Report to see if your email address has appeared in known data breaches. This can help you prioritize which passwords to change.

5. Watch for Phishing Scams+

Be extra vigilant. Criminals will use this data to create convincing fake emails and texts. Never click suspicious links. If in doubt, go directly to the company's official website.

6. Scan Your Devices for Malware+

Since infostealer malware is the root cause, ensure your computer and phone have reputable, up-to-date antivirus software. Run a full scan to find and remove any threats.
